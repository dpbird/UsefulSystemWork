Consider the game described in Hint 1. The social optimum cost in this game is $\text{OPT} = 1+ \epsilon$,
corresponding to the solution in which all players choose $e_0$, each incurring a cost of $(1+\epsilon)/k>1/k$.

Also it is a pure-strategy Nash equilibrium for each player $i$ to choose $\{e_i\}$, resulting in social cost of $\sum_{i=1}^{n}1/i = H_n$.

For the sake of contradiction, say that there exists another pure-Nash equilibrium, in which  $k \geq 1$ players choose $e_0$. However, notice that $e_1, \ldots, e_{k-1}$ are the only resources that have base cost greater than $1/k$. So for at least one of the $k$ players, there must exist at least one player $i \in \{k, \ldots, n\}$, whose cost for choosing resource $\{e_i\}$ is strictly smaller than their current cost when choosing $\{e_0\}$. So they have an incentive to deviate to $\{e_i\}$. So this cannot be an equilibrium. So the PoS $=\frac{H_n}{1+\epsilon} = \Omega(\log n)$, by Hint 2.

Since the Price of Anarchy for a class of games is the supremum over all games in the class, it suffices to exhibit a single fair cost-sharing game achieving $PoA = \Omega(n).$
Consider a game with two resources $e_0$ and $e_1$ where each player $i \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$ may choose either resource $e_0$ or resource $e_1$, that is, for all $i$ we have $S_i = \{\{e_0\}, \{e_1\}\}$. Resource $e_0$ has $1+\epsilon$ and resource $e_1$ has base cost $n$. All players choosing $s_i = \{e_1\}$ is a Nash equilibrium since each player is incurring cost $n/n = 1$ and has no incentive to deviate. But this has social cost $n$, compared to the optimal social cost of $1+\epsilon$.








Let $s$ be a pure-strategy Nash Equilibrium. Let $r^*$ be the resource with the highest load, such that $L_{r^*}(s) = Cost(s)$. Let $i^*$ be any player assigned to resource $r^*$ (i.e., $s_{i^*} = r^*$). Prove that for any other resource $r \in R \setminus \{r^*\}$, the load $L_r(s)$ must satisfy $L_r(s) \ge L_{r^*}(s) - w_{i^*}$.









In the Nash Equilibrium $s$, player $i^*$ chooses resource $r^*$. Player $i^*$ could unilaterally deviate to any other resource $r$. If they did, the new load on $r\not=r^*$ would become $L_r(s) + w_{i^*}$. 
    
Because $s$ is a Nash Equilibrium, player $i^*$ has no incentive to deviate, meaning their current cost must be less than or equal to the cost of deviation:
$$L_{r^*}(s) \le L_r(s) + w_{i^*} \implies L_r(s) \ge L_{r^*}(s) - w_{i^*}$$  









Let $OPT$ be the optimal social cost. We know two lower bounds for $OPT$:\\
$OPT \ge \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i} w_i$ (the optimal max-load cannot be less than the average load),\\ and $OPT \ge \max_i w_i$ (the optimal max-load cannot be less than the largest single weight).

Note that the inequality from part (a) was proved for $r \neq r^*$, but the case $r = r^*$ holds trivially since $L_{r^*}(s) \ge L_{r^*}(s) - w_{i^*}$ is always true for positive weights. Thus we may sum the inequality over all $m$ resources:
$$ \sum_{r=1}^m L_r(s) \ge \sum_{r=1}^m (L_{r^*}(s) - w_{i^*}) $$
Denoting the total weight as $W = \sum_i w_i$:
$$ W \ge m \cdot L_{r^*}(s) - m \cdot w_{i^*} $$
$$ \frac{W}{m} + w_{i^*} \ge L_{r^*}(s) $$
Since $L_{r^*}(s) = Cost(s)$, and substituting the bounds for $OPT$:
$$ Cost(s) \le \frac{W}{m} + w_{i^*} \le OPT + OPT = 2 \cdot OPT $$
Thus, $PoA \le 2$.
